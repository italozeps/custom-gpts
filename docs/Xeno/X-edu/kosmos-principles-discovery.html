Carlos E. Perez reviews a landmark scientific paper describing Kosmos, an AI system capable of performing at a scale and depth previously unimaginable for automated research. Instead of basic data analysis, Kosmos uses a live-updating, structured world model—essentially a digital “shared consciousness”—to coordinate hundreds of agents working in parallel, each specializing in literature, code, or empirical analysis. Its workflow generates thousands of lines of code, reads thousands of papers, and produces fully traceable results.

Kosmos delivers new, reproducible scientific discoveries, not just summaries. Every claim is hyperlinked to primary papers or exact code, dramatically improving transparency and verifiability compared to “black box” AI science. Major findings have included solutions to problems in neuroscience and medical research, reproducing unpublished discoveries and methods.

Kosmos fundamentally shifts our understanding of what scientific AI can achieve. By organizing agents to share a “global whiteboard”—a structure allowing full context and memory continuity—it enables research projects that previously required months or years of coordinated human effort to be completed in hours.

Key innovations include evolutionary search, knowledge synthesis, and collaborative organization—principles identified across analyzed systems (AlphaEvolve, Kosmos, LCoF Encyclopedia, AsyncThink). These paradigms, along with universal traits like verifiability and feedback-driven iteration, support resilient, transparent, cross-disciplinary research.

Kosmos highlights a future where scientific progress is incredibly rapid, transparent, and democratized. Single researchers, aided by agentic systems, may soon produce breakthroughs formerly reserved for whole institutions, making the cycle of discovery “very, very weird”—and vastly more dynamic.
